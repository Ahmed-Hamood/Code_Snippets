Current, Voltage and Resistance overview

1. Current, Amperes and Amps

Current is the flow of electrons. When a circuit is closed then a current of electrons can flow and when a circuit is open then no current can flow.

Electric current refers to the flow of electricity in an electronic circuit, and to the amount of electricity flowing through a circuit. It is measured in amperes (A). The larger the value in amperes, the more electricity is flowing in the circuit.

We can measure the flow of electrons just like you can measure the flow of water through a pipe. To measure the flow of electrons we use the unit of Amperes or Amps for short.

1 Amp means 1 Coulomb per second and one coulomb equals 6,242,000,000,000,000,000 electrons per second.

This is an extremely large number which is why they are grouped together and called Amps.

Current is the rate at which electrons flow past a point in a complete electrical circuit. We use Amps to measure current, it expresses the quantity of electrons or electrical charge flowing past a point in a circuit over given time

Electrons are negatively charged they flow from (-) negative terminal to the positive (+) terminal.

A current of 1 ampere means that 1 coulomb of electrons—that's 6.24 billion billion (6.24 x 1018)

Here's some symbols used for amps :-

  • A = amperes, for a large amount of current (1.000).
  • mA = milli-amperes, a thousandth of an amp (0.001).
  • µA = micro-amperes, a millionth of an amp (0.000001).

The amount number of the electron charges that travel or flow thru the wire. The amount of charges passing per unit time is the current. After the current pass the resistance the output current will be less voltage (energy), and less electron charges (current). The higher the resistance the less current (charge flow decrease) going to be

2. Volts and Voltage

Voltage is a pushing force of electrons in a circuit, think of voltage like pressure in a water pipe. The more pressure you have the more water can flow. The more voltage you have the more electrons can flow.

A Volt is Joule per Coulomb A Joule is a measurement of energy or work. A Coulomb is a group of flowing electrons. Heres the formula.

A 9V battery can provide 9 Joules of energy, in the form of work or heat, per group of electrons that flow from one terminal of the battery to the other.

In this case the electrons from one terminal of the battery, through the L.E.D bulb and then into the other terminal of the battery. Therefore 9 Joules of light and heat is produced by the bulb.

The potential energy of the electrons charges or the amount of pressure on these charges thru wire. Higher voltage mean higher amount of current (electron charges) will travel thru the wire.

3. Resistance and Resistors

Resistance is a restriction to the flow of electrons in a circuit. Every wire that curries current will naturally have some resistance. Each wire material has different resistance to the flow of electrons.

Resistance is measured in ohms Ω - Resistance consist of materials, that pushed the electrons back against the current flow

Resistance is used to decrease the electrons charges or Lower/Drop the voltage or decrease the potential energy of the electron charge. Mean lower potential energy and less current (electron charges), after hitting the resistance the energy of the electron charge has been decreased that means the voltage is decreased;

  • The longer the wire the greater the resistance. (less current flow).
  • The thicker the wire the less the resistance. (more current flow).
  • Copper wire has less resistance than the aluminum wire
  • Temperature will effect the level of resistance.

Resistance occurs when electrons collide with atoms. The number of collisions varies by material, some materials such as Iron will have a very high rate of collisions whereas other materials such as copper has much fewer collisions.

When collisions occur the atoms generate heat and at a certain temperature the material will then start to produce light as well as heat, which is how the incandescent lamps work.

We need resistance in our circuit to limit current flow and to reduce collision, so our light doesn't burn out. Our light only can handle a certain temperature or collision level, too much current mean too much collision therefor too much heat.

- Resistor

Resistors add resistance to the circuit and reduces the flow of electrical current/electrons. The purpose of using resistor is to protect other electrical components from receiving too much current.

Always keep in mind that electricity always follows the path of least resistance to ground. read more

Also note that a switch does not add any resistance to a circuit and simply adding a switch between power and ground will create a short circuit.

You should keep in mind is that electricity in a circuit must be used. In other word you should provide something wired between positive (+) and ground (-) that adds resistance to the flow of electricity and uses it up.

There needs to be something wired between positive and ground that adds resistance to the flow of electricity and uses it up.

If positive voltage is connected directly to ground and does not first pass through something that adds resistance, like a motor, this will result in a short circuit or wire overheating as you see below.

# This means that the positive voltage is connected directly to ground or no resistance is applied :-

It is very important to prevent short circuits by making sure that the positive voltage is never wired directly to ground.

4. Calculate Current and Power

Power is how much energy we used within a period of time. Less resistance, more voltage and more current means more power.

- Example 1

We have battery with 5v attach to wire that has a resistance of 100 Ohms. Calculate the amount of current (electron charges) will pass ?

  • Voltage (V) = 5V
  • Resistance (R) = 100 Ohms
  • Current (I) = ?? Amps
  • Calculating the current is dividing voltage by resistance:
  • I = V / R ➤ I = 5/100 ➤ I = 0.05Amps

- Example 2

DC power supply with 0.05Amps output current and 10V output voltage, whats the power this device can supply?

  • Voltage (V) = 10V
  • Current (I) = 0.05Amps
  • Power (P) = ?? W
  • Calculate the power is multiply voltage with current
  • P = V x I ➤ P = 10 x 0.05 ➤ P = 1W
