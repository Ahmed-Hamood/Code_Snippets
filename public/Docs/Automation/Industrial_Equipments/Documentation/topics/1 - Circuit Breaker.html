Circuit Breaker

1. What is Circuit breaker ?

A circuit breaker is an automatically or manually operated electrical switch designed to protect an electrical circuit from damage caused by excess current from an overload or short circuit.

A circuit breaker is an electrical device that provides protection against fault current. It breaks the circuit in case of overloading & short circuit. The fault currents generated due to these fault conditions can damage the electrical devices as well as cause fire in a building that can also pose danger to human life.

You will find a circuit breaker in every automation control panel.

The circuit breaker is a switching device which also offers protection by tripping and cutting off the supply to load in case of fault.

Its like fuse but in circuit breaker it will only trip so we can turn on again no need to replace anything. The circuit breaker instantly cut off the power supply to reduce further damage.

- These conditions are defined as FAULTS :-

  • Overload current: possible causes for over-current include short circuits, excessive load, incorrect design, an arc fault, or a ground fault. Overload occurs when you exceed the current that your circuit breaker is setup with. Also occur when your load dis-function which lead to high current withdraw that will cause overload to your circuit.
  • Short Circuit: occurs when a “hot” wire carrying live current comes into contact with a neutral wire, either by touching it directly or through arcing—allowing the current to jump from the live wire to the neutral one.
  • Earth Leakage: when there is an electrical connection between the ground and a conductor, which has not been designed intentionally.

overload is just an excess of current—or amperage—in an electrical circuit. An over-current occurs when the current exceeds the rated amperage capacity of that circuit or of the connected equipment (such as an appliance) on that circuit. An over-current can be caused by overloading the circuit or by a short circuit, a ground fault, or an arc fault. Circuit breakers and fuses protect circuit wiring from damage caused by over-current.

The best ways to prevent leakage current are to always use shielded cables on motor installations and to make sure that these cables are grounded at both ends. Also, check cables and insulation for damage and replace ones that are broken or damaged.

A circuit breaker has two types of tripping unit, it has thermal and magnetic tripping unit.

Thermal Tripping Unit: is used for protection against overloading. It uses a bi-metallic contact that bends with a change in temperature. The current flowing through the bimetallic strip heats up contact & trip the circuit breaker.

The rate of bending of the bi-metallic strip depends on the amount of current. Therefore, greater the overloading current, faster the circuit breaker trips.

Magnetic Tripping Unit: is used for protection against short circuit current. It includes a solenoid that produced a strong magnetic field due to high short circuit current to instantly trip the circuit breaker.

A - MCB - Miniature Circuit Breaker

MCB is used at homes to protect home appliances, and also it is used in industrial automation control panel. MCB can be used as a switch to turn ON/OFF Loads

MCB will be effected by a short circuit, overload or imperfect design. MCB Circuit Breaker can take up to 100 Amps Maximum which protect from an over-current - overheat protection.

Let imagine that an excessive current passing thru your cable. Let say you cable size is small can't handle over-current, this cable eventually going to heat up and cause a fire. So we use circuit breaker to protect from overheat but it will take seconds or minutes to act or trip

B - MCCB - Molded Case Circuit Breaker

The MCCB is used to control electric energy in distribution and in the main input power of large control panel. It has short circuit and overload protection, and it can handle rated current upto 1000 Amps (1KAmper).

The characteristics of an MCCB mainly include the following :-

  • The range of rated current us up to 1000 amperes
  • Trip current may be adjusted
  • Thermal magnetic operation

C - ELCB - Earth Leakage Circuit Breaker

You can find it on panel that is distribute power to your automation panels and it protect the whole factory. It monitors the three phases and monitor the current that is going back thru the neutral

The ELCB senses any earth leakage like any one touching the phase line by mistake then a leakage current is going to pass thru that phase line to the ground causing the ELCB to detect a leakage so it will trip cutting power so fast within millisecond to prevent serious hazards

The ELCB is used to protect the circuit from the electrical leakage. When someone gets an electric shock, then this circuit breaker cuts off the power at the time of 0.1 secs for protecting the personal safety and avoiding the gear from the circuit against short circuit and overload.

The characteristics of an ELCB mainly include the following :-

  • This circuit breaker connects the phase, earth wire and neutral

3. Circuit breaker Trip Curve

Trip Curve is about circuit protection from potential hazards that may occurs in your electrical panel at any time. Here some problems that will cause circuit breaker to trip.

  • Short circuit: Device may malfunction (fail) causing a short circuit (device burn because of a hot wire cause by over-current)
  • Over-current: Main power source may have problem it will Send a high voltage (high current) to your equipment
  • Overload: problem occur on loads like motor may get stack and withdraw more current than it needs then it'll end up burned

Trip curve data-sheet graph is available on every circuit breaker, it explains how long a circuit breaker will take at a certain current to Trip

Trip curve graph has two variable Time duration and Rated Current X n (6 or 10 times)

Don't think it will trip when it reaches 10 Amps. No, it will take time to trip

It will take time because most of Circuit Breaker are thermal protection devices which mean it will take time to trip because of current overload. When it reaches a certain amount of current it will take a certain amount of time until it trips, so its not an immediate reaction.

Circuit Breaker with thermal protection, the rated current usually is multiply by X1 times only. So if your circuit breaker has 10 Amps rated current. here an example

some circuit breaker has a magnetic protection which trip instantly, when a short circuit occurs this type of circuit breaker protection will trip immediately. This current usually is in between X6 and X10 times larger than the rated current of that circuit breaker.

Trip curve graph has a two sections for thermal response and short circuit (magnetic) response which mean our circuit breaker has both protection thermal and magnetic response

Thermal response as you see the slow portion response (more delay), and Short circuit or magnetic response it has a fast portion response (less delay) almost an immediate tripping

The big question why we need a delay in a thermal protection here why?

In-Rush Current last for few second or few hundred milliseconds in order to give an over-current to our loads (motor) the circuit breaker will give a chance to the load, to withdraw its In-Rush Current to start successfully.

Again the circuit breaker will give a few seconds chance for inrush current to pass without cutting the power
